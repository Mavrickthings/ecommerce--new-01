How to Choose Your First Quality Watch (Without Breaking the Bank)

By Jordan Lee· June 8, 2025· 7 min read

A watch is one of the few accessories that men and women across almost every culture have worn for over a century. In an age where your phone tells the time, a watch says something about who you are — your relationship with craft, precision, and the tactile pleasures of well-made things.

The problem: watch marketing is notoriously confusing, and the price range is absurd — from $30 to $300,000. Here's how to navigate it sensibly and find a first watch you'll actually wear for years.

Step 1: Understand the Movement

The movement is the engine of the watch. There are three types that matter for most buyers:

Quartz

Battery-powered, extremely accurate (±15 seconds per month), low maintenance, and affordable. The majority of everyday watches use quartz movements. They're practical, reliable, and entirely appropriate for a first watch. Don't let anyone tell you quartz is inferior — it's just different.

Best for: Daily wear, outdoor activities, first-time buyers.

Japanese Automatic

Self-winding through wrist movement, no battery required. Brands like Seiko and Orient offer exceptional value in the $100–$400 range. The caseback often features an exhibition window to show the movement — a genuine pleasure if you appreciate mechanical things.

Best for: Enthusiasts who enjoy the craft; those who want a watch that runs forever with proper care.

Swiss Mechanical

The prestige tier. Swiss-made movements from ETA, Sellita, or in-house manufacturers command premium prices. For a first watch, you don't need this — but it's worth knowing where the ceiling is.

Step 2: Understand Case Materials

The case (and bracelet, if applicable) determines durability, weight, and aesthetics:

  • Stainless Steel (316L or 904L): The gold standard for everyday watches. Durable, hypoallergenic, and develops no patina. Most quality watches in the $50–$500 range use 316L steel.
  • Mineral Crystal: Functional glass. Will scratch over years of use. Fine for budget watches.
  • Sapphire Crystal: Second hardest material after diamond. Sapphire glass resists scratching almost completely. A key quality indicator — look for it on watches $150 and above.
  • Titanium: Lighter than steel, equally durable. Often found on sport and field watches.

Step 3: Water Resistance — What the Numbers Mean

This is the most misunderstood spec in watchmaking. The ratings are not what they seem:

  • 30m / 3ATM: Splash resistant only. Not for swimming.
  • 50m / 5ATM: Short swims okay. No diving or high-pressure water.
  • 100m / 10ATM: Swimming, snorkeling. Most lifestyle watches target this rating.
  • 200m+ / 20ATM+: Actual diving watches.

The key insight: ratings are tested in static conditions, not dynamic ones. A 50m-rated watch taken for a vigorous swim may admit water. When in doubt, size up by one rating category for your activity.

Buy for the life you live, not the life you imagine living. If you rarely swim, 50m water resistance is plenty. If you're a daily swimmer, get 200m minimum.

Step 4: Case Diameter and Your Wrist

Watch sizing is deeply personal, but there are starting points:

  • Under 38mm: Dress watches, vintage-inspired designs. Suits smaller wrists elegantly.
  • 38–42mm: The versatile middle ground. Works on most wrist sizes for most occasions.
  • 42–46mm: Sport and field watches. Bold, modern. May overwhelm narrow wrists.
  • 46mm+: Statement pieces. Deliberate and specific.

The most important measure is lug-to-lug width — the distance from top to bottom of the case across your wrist. Look this up for any watch you're seriously considering.

Final Thoughts

Your first quality watch doesn't need to be expensive. It needs to be well-made, honestly described, and appropriate for how you actually live.
